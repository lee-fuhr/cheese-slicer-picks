Cheese Slicer questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Cheese slicers come in two main types: wire and blade. Your choice depends on cheese texture, soft cheeses need a thin wire, hard cheeses a sharp blade. This guide answers the most common questions to help you pick the right one.

How do wire and blade cheese slicers differ?

Wire cheese slicers use a taut, thin metal wire to glide through soft to semi-hard cheeses. They are ideal for cheeses like mozzarella, brie, or fresh chèvre, where a clean cut without sticking matters. Blade slicers, sometimes called cheese planes, rely on a sharp, slotted stainless steel blade that shaves thin slices off harder cheeses. Blade slicers are the workhorses for aged gouda, cheddar, or provolone. They create even slices with less drag than a wire. Many home cooks own both types because cheese texture varies so much. A wire slicer gives you a gentle cut; a blade slicer excels at firmer blocks. If you mostly eat soft cheeses, a wire slicer will serve you best. If hard cheeses dominate your fridge, a blade model is more practical. Some combination tools exist, but specialized slicers often perform better at their intended job.

Which cheese slicer works best for hard cheeses like parmesan?

Hard and very hard cheeses, parmesan, pecorino, or aged manchego, benefit most from a blade-style slicer with a long, sharp cutting surface. A cheese plane with a slightly curved blade helps you apply even pressure along the block, producing thin, intact slices without crumbling. Wire slicers can struggle with ultra-hard cheese because the wire may snap or warp under the pressure needed. Some heavy-duty wire slicers with thicker gauge wire work, but they are less common. For parmesan, a sturdy blade slicer with a comfortable handle is the trusted choice among reviewers and serious cooks. Look for a slicer with a blade that is easy to sharpen or replace. Stainless steel blades hold an edge well, and many affordable models offer surprising longevity. Avoid plastic frames for heavy use; a metal or reinforced frame provides stability when pressing through dense cheese.

Can you use a cheese slicer on soft cheeses like brie?

Yes, but you need the right tool. A thin wire slicer is the best option for soft, creamy cheeses. The wire slides through without mashing or sticking, preserving the delicate texture and rind. Wire slicers often have a wooden base that catches the cheese, making transfer to a plate easy. Blade slicers tend to push or smear soft cheese because the blade lacks the fine, tensioned edge needed. If you only have a blade slicer, chilling the cheese briefly can help, but it is not ideal. For brie, camembert, or fresh goat cheese, a dedicated wire slicer is worth the modest investment. Some wire slicers come with an adjustable arm so you can vary slice thickness. For soft cheese, a slightly thicker slice–around a quarter inch–holds together better than paper-thin ones. Thinner slices work for garnishes or melting, but be prepared for some sticking.

What should you look for in a cheese slicer frame?

The frame determines how easy and comfortable the slicer is to use. Look for a design that keeps your hand away from the wire or blade during operation. A guard or a raised handle is a simple safety feature that many budget-friendly models include. Materials matter. Stainless steel or aluminum frames resist rust and stay sturdy over years of use. Wooden frames look classic and feel warm but require hand-washing and occasional oiling. Plastic frames are lightweight and dishwasher-safe, though they may crack under heavy pressure with hard cheeses. Ergonomics also count. A frame with a non-slip base or suction feet prevents the slicer from sliding on the countertop. If you prepare cheese platters often, a longer frame lets you slice larger blocks in one pass. Compact frames suit smaller kitchens or occasional use.

How do you clean and maintain a cheese slicer?

Almost all cheese slicers are easy to clean if you do it promptly. Wire slicers: rinse the wire under hot water and wipe the frame with a damp cloth. Avoid abrasive scrubbers that can scratch metal or wood. For stuck cheese, a brief soak in warm soapy water works, but do not submerge any wooden parts for long. Blade slicers should be washed by hand with mild detergent and dried immediately. The blade’s slot can trap cheese particles; use a soft brush or toothpick to clear them. Many blade slicers are dishwasher-safe, but high heat can dull the edge over time. Check the manufacturer’s care instructions. Occasional blade sharpening keeps performance high. A few passes on a fine steel or ceramic rod restores the edge. Wire slicer wires rarely need replacement unless they snap; keep a spare wire on hand for quick swaps. Oiling wooden frames monthly prolongs their life.

Are adjustable cheese slicers worth it?

Adjustable thickness settings are a genuine convenience if you slice cheese for different uses. A model with a simple sliding arm or tension knob lets you switch from thin shavings for crackers to thicker slices for sandwiches. This flexibility reduces the need for multiple slicers. The trade-off is complexity. Adjustable mechanisms can loosen over time or become stiff if cheese residue gets inside. Seek out models with straightforward adjustment that clicks firmly into place. Metal gears and a robust frame are signs of a well-made adjustable slicer. For someone who serves cheese often or likes variety, an adjustable slicer is a smart purchase. If you mostly cut one type of cheese at a consistent thickness, a fixed-setting slicer is simpler, less expensive, and equally reliable.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best cheese slicer for cheddar?

A blade-style cheese plane works very well for medium to sharp cheddar. It gives you control over thickness and produces clean, even slices without crumbling. A wire slicer can also work on softer young cheddar, but the blade is more versatile.

Do wire slicers rust?

High-quality wire slicers use stainless steel wire that resists rust. However, if the wire is not dried after washing or if it is stored in a damp environment, surface rust can appear. Wipe the wire dry after each use and store the slicer in a dry place.

Can a cheese slicer cut butter?

Yes, both wire and blade slicers can cut cold butter. A wire slicer works especially well for butter straight from the fridge because the thin wire doesn’t stick as much as a blade. For softened butter, a blade slicer may smear, just let the butter firm up first.

How thick should cheese slices be?

The ideal thickness depends on the cheese and its use. For snacking, slices between 1/8 and 1/4 inch are common. Thin slices (1/16 inch) work well for sandwiches or layering, while thicker cuts (1/2 inch) suit grilled cheese or cheese boards where texture stands out.

Are cheese slicers dishwasher safe?

Many blade and wire slicers with plastic or stainless steel frames are labeled dishwasher safe, but hand washing is gentler and extends the life of the blade or wire. Always check the product’s care instructions before putting any slicer in the dishwasher.

What's the difference between a cheese plane and a cheese slicer?

The terms are often used interchangeably, but a cheese plane usually refers to a blade-style tool with a slot that shaves slices, while a cheese slicer can include both wire and blade types. In practice, a cheese plane is a specific kind of slicer designed for hard cheeses.

In shortChoosing a cheese slicer comes down to the cheeses you most often eat and how much variety you want. A wire slicer handles soft cheeses beautifully; a blade slicer masters hard cheeses. Adjustable models offer flexibility, and simple fixed slicers are reliable workhorses. Whichever you pick, clean it well and it will serve you for years.
