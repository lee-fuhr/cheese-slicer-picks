How to choose the right cheese slicer

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best cheese slicer for you depends on the cheeses you eat most often. Wire slicers handle soft cheeses gracefully, while classic paddle slicers excel with firm blocks. Focus on build quality, ergonomics, and easy cleaning, not flashy features.

What type of cheese slicer do you actually need?

Cheese slicers generally fall into three categories: wire slicers, paddle slicers (often called cheese planes), and adjustable-blade slicers. Wire slicers use a taut stainless-steel wire to cut through soft to semi-soft cheeses without crushing them, ideal for brie, camembert, and fresh mozzarella. Paddle slicers have a slotted metal blade that shaves thin, even slices off hard cheeses like cheddar or gouda. Adjustable slicers let you set slice thickness, typically from paper-thin to about a quarter-inch. They are versatile but can be bulkier and harder to clean than simpler designs. The right choice depends on your cheese-eating habits: if you primarily buy soft-ripened cheeses, a wire slicer is your best friend. If hard blocks and wedges dominate your fridge, a paddle slicer will serve you better.

Which cheese slicer works best for soft versus hard cheeses?

For soft cheeses (brie, camembert, chèvre), a wire slicer is the clear winner. The thin wire cuts cleanly through creamy interiors without smearing or sticking, and the resulting slice holds its shape beautifully. Many wire slicers also include a grippy handle and a stable base that prevents the cheese from shifting. For hard and semi-hard cheeses (aged gouda, parmesan, cheddar), a paddle slicer gives you thin, even slices with no crumbling. The best models have a sharp, angled blade and a comfortable handle. Some dual-purpose slicers combine a wire and a paddle blade on opposite ends, but these often sacrifice ergonomics for versatility. If you eat a mix of textures, consider buying two dedicated tools instead of one compromise.

Why stainless steel matters for cheese slicers

The blade or wire is the heart of any cheese slicer, and stainless steel is the material to look for. It resists corrosion from cheese acids, stays sharp longer than plated steel, and won’t rust even after countless washes. High-carbon stainless steel holds an edge especially well, though it may require slightly more care to avoid staining. Cheaper slicers sometimes use chrome-plated steel or aluminum. These can be fine for occasional use, but they dull faster and may react with acidic cheeses like aged goat cheese. A fully stainless-steel slicer (blade and frame) will last for years and is worth the few extra dollars it typically costs.

How to choose the right size and handle design

Cheese slicers come in a range of sizes, from compact three-inch paddles to full-length twelve-inch wires. A larger blade or wire lets you cut larger wheels or blocks in a single pass, which is convenient if you entertain often. But a smaller slicer is easier to store and maneuver, especially for quick snacks. Handle design is just as important. Look for a comfortable, non-slip grip that fits your hand size. Many popular models use soft-touch rubber or contoured plastic. Avoid handles that are too narrow or slippery; they can cause hand fatigue during prolonged use. A slightly curved or angled handle often provides better leverage for clean cuts.

Can you put a cheese slicer in the dishwasher?

It depends on the materials. Many stainless-steel wire slicers are dishwasher-safe, the wire and frame handle high temperatures well. However, the high heat and harsh detergents can eventually dull blades or loosen tension wires. For paddle slicers with wooden or plastic handles, hand washing is almost always recommended to prevent cracking or warping. Even for dishwasher-safe models, hand drying after the cycle will extend the life of the blade. Cheese slicers with intricate crevices (adjustable mechanisms, hinged parts) tend to trap food particles. A quick rinse and scrub with a soft brush usually cleans them more thoroughly than a dishwasher can.

Frequently asked questions

What’s the difference between a cheese plane and a cheese slicer?

The terms are often used interchangeably, but strictly speaking, a cheese plane is a paddle-style slicer with a slotted blade that you pull across a block. A cheese slicer can refer to any tool that cuts cheese, including wire models. In practice, most people call both a cheese slicer.

Can I use a cheese slicer on soft cheese like brie?

Yes, but use a wire slicer rather than a paddle. A paddle tends to smear soft cheese. A thin stainless wire cuts through cleanly without crushing the interior. Some wire slicers also have a dedicated frame that holds even very soft wedges steady.

How do I clean a cheese slicer without damaging the wire?

Rinse the wire under warm water immediately after use, using a soft sponge or brush. Avoid abrasive pads that can scratch the wire. For stubborn residue, soak the slicer in warm soapy water for a few minutes, then rinse. Dry thoroughly with a soft cloth to prevent water spots.

Are adjustable cheese slicers worth it?

They can be useful if you regularly switch between cheese types and want consistent thickness. However, many adjustable models have more parts that can break or become difficult to clean. A simple, fixed-thickness slicer is often more durable and easier to maintain. Consider an adjustable only if you truly need varied slice thicknesses.

Should I buy a cheese slicer with a swivel blade?

A swivel blade helps the slicer follow the natural curve of a cheese wedge, producing more even slices. It’s a nice feature for round or irregularly shaped cheeses. But for straight blocks, a fixed blade cuts just as well. Swivel mechanisms add a potential failure point, so ensure it’s made by a reputable brand.

What cheese slicer do professional cheesemongers use?

Many cheesemongers prefer a classic stainless-steel paddle slicer from Boska or a high-end wire slicer from OXO Good Grips. They value durability, sharpness, and a comfortable grip for long hours behind the counter. They often avoid adjustable slicers because of cleaning hassles in a busy shop.

In shortA thoughtfully chosen cheese slicer makes every cheese board more enjoyable. Prioritize stainless steel, a comfortable grip, and a style that matches your cheese-eating habits. Skip the gimmicks, a simple, well-made tool will serve you for years.
